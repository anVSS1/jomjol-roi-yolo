\begin{abstract}
Automated Meter Reading (AMR) is an important component of smart infrastructure, enabling utility providers to digitize meter readings without replacing existing hardware. However, many practical edge-based solutions still depend on manual Region of Interest (ROI) configuration, which limits scalability and increases deployment effort. This paper proposes a fully automated distributed AMR pipeline that removes this bottleneck through a two-stage architecture: a lightweight YOLOv8n detector automatically localizes the meter digit strip on Machine~A, and a custom CNN classifies segmented digits on Machine~B through TCP communication.

To organize the discussion, the paper follows the LBC matrix: \textit{Literature} reviews prior AMR approaches and identifies the limitations of manual-ROI workflows, \textit{Business need} motivates the need for an autonomous and scalable system, and \textit{Contribution} presents the proposed distributed architecture and its experimental validation. The model achieves a 100\% ROI detection success rate on the local test set and 99.00\% hold-out accuracy for digit classification. End-to-end evaluation on an independent dataset reaches a 72.00\% exact full-reading match, showing that the proposed system is effective but still limited by recognition errors under difficult lighting and tilt conditions.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} Automated Meter Reading, YOLOv8, CNN, Distributed Inference, Edge Computing, ROI Detection.

\section{Introduction}
The growth of smart cities and connected infrastructure has increased the need for reliable and low-cost methods of collecting utility data. Automated Meter Reading (AMR) addresses this need by extracting numeric readings from analog water, gas, or electricity meters using computer vision rather than manual inspection. In practice, however, many systems still depend on human-assisted setup, especially when the digit area must be manually defined before inference can begin.

This work is structured according to the \textbf{LBC matrix}, which separates the study into three parts. The \textbf{L}iterature axis summarizes prior work and highlights current technical limitations, the \textbf{B}usiness axis defines the operational need for a scalable and autonomous solution, and the \textbf{C}ontribution axis presents the proposed distributed pipeline and its evaluation.

\section{L-Axis: Literature and State of the Art}

\subsection{Early AMR Approaches}
Traditional AMR systems often relied on classical image processing methods such as thresholding, edge detection, contour extraction, and morphological filtering to identify the meter window or digit strip. These methods are computationally efficient, but they are highly sensitive to illumination changes, shadows, reflections, camera motion, and meter degradation. As a result, they tend to work only under controlled conditions.

\subsection{Deep Learning for Meter Reading}
Recent progress in deep learning has significantly improved object detection and recognition tasks. In particular, YOLO-based detectors offer a strong trade-off between speed and accuracy, which makes them suitable for real-time or edge-oriented applications. For meter reading, detection models can be used to localize the ROI automatically, while CNN-based classifiers can recognize individual digits after segmentation.

\subsection{Relevant Existing Work}
Several recent studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of learning-based AMR systems. Laroca et al.~\cite{laroca2019} introduced a CNN-based pipeline for automatic meter reading, showing that deep models can replace hand-crafted feature engineering in practical meter analysis. In a later study, Laroca et al.~\cite{laroca2021} proposed a robust image-based AMR system for unconstrained scenarios, highlighting the advantages of deep learning for meter localization and reading under real-world conditions. Related work on analog dial meters also explored deep learning for automatic reading, including YOLO-based detection and regression-oriented estimation strategies for unconstrained dial-meter scenarios~\cite{laroca2020dial}. Together, these studies confirm the value of data-driven approaches while also motivating lighter and more deployment-oriented architectures.

\subsection{Limitations of Manual ROI Setups}
Despite these advances, some practical edge deployments still require manual ROI configuration. In such workflows, the user must draw the digit strip manually during installation, and the system becomes fragile if the camera position changes later. This limitation reduces deployment scalability and increases maintenance cost. The legacy AI-on-the-Edge workflow is a representative example of this issue, since it relies on human-defined bounding regions instead of fully automatic localization.

\section{B-Axis: Business Need and Problem Statement}

\subsection{Scalability Requirement}
From an industrial perspective, large-scale AMR deployment must minimize human intervention. A utility provider managing thousands of endpoints cannot afford repeated manual setup or recalibration for each camera installation. Therefore, the system must detect the reading region automatically and remain robust to small shifts in camera angle, distance, or alignment.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The main question addressed in this paper is:
\begin{quote}
How can manual ROI configuration be eliminated in edge-based AMR systems to build a fully autonomous and scalable reading pipeline?
\end{quote}

\subsection{Operational Requirements}
To satisfy this need, the proposed system should:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item Detect the digit region automatically without manual bounding-box setup.
    \item Read individual characters accurately after cropping and segmentation.
    \item Operate in a distributed mode where image acquisition and ROI localization can be separated from digit classification.
    \item Remain lightweight enough to support practical deployment on constrained hardware.
\end{enumerate}

\section{C-Axis: Contribution and System Design}

\subsection{Overview of the Proposed Pipeline}
To address the problem, we propose a distributed AMR architecture composed of two communicating nodes. Machine~A performs image acquisition and ROI detection using a lightweight YOLOv8n model, then transmits the cropped digit strip to Machine~B over a TCP socket. Machine~B receives the ROI, segments the digits using a geometry-based approach, and classifies each one with a custom CNN. This separation reduces the inference burden on the acquisition node while preserving modularity and flexibility.

\subsection{Distributed Architecture}
The system is organized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Machine A (Client):} captures the meter image, detects the ROI with YOLOv8n (with rotation retry and OpenCV fallback), crops the digit strip, JPEG-encodes it, and transmits the bytes to Machine~B via a length-prefixed TCP protocol.
    \item \textbf{Machine B (Server):} receives and decodes the JPEG ROI, splits it into five equal vertical strips, classifies each strip with a CNN, and returns the reconstructed reading string to Machine~A.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Automatic ROI Detection with YOLOv8}
A YOLOv8n model was trained to detect a single class, namely \textit{counter\_roi}, using data adapted from the UFPR-AMR dataset. Reducing the detection problem to a single class simplifies training and improves convergence while preserving robustness in unconstrained scenes. The model achieved the following validation results:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{mAP@50:} 99.34\%
    \item \textbf{mAP@50-95:} 75.62\%
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rotation-Retry and Fallback Detection}
To improve robustness under camera tilt, Machine~A implements a two-level fallback strategy executed entirely before any data is transmitted to Machine~B.

\textbf{Level 1 â€” Rotation retry.}
If YOLOv8n fails to detect any ROI on the original frame, the image is iteratively rotated using eight candidate angles: $[+10^\circ,\,-10^\circ,\,+20^\circ,\,-20^\circ,\,+30^\circ,\,-30^\circ,\,+45^\circ,\,-45^\circ]$. Each rotated view is tested independently. Rotation is performed with \texttt{cv2.warpAffine} using a full-canvas expansion (so no content is cropped), and the detected bounding box in the rotated frame is mapped back to the original coordinate system via the inverse affine transform. Crucially, when a rotated attempt succeeds, the ROI crop is extracted from the rotated frame itself, ensuring that the digit strip arrives at Machine~B in an upright orientation that is consistent with the CNN training distribution.

\textbf{Level 2 â€” OpenCV geometric fallback.}
If YOLO finds no ROI across all rotated views, a classical fallback estimates the counter window using morphological gradient filtering followed by Otsu binarization and contour analysis. Candidate rectangles are scored by area and aspect ratio (requiring a minimum ratio of 2.5:1 and a minimum width of 15\% of the frame), selecting the largest qualifying landscape region. A secondary pass using Canny edge detection is applied if the gradient pass returns no valid candidate.

This layered approach ensures that even in degraded conditions, a best-effort ROI is always transmitted rather than silently discarding the frame.

\subsection{Geometry-Based Digit Segmentation on Machine~B}
After receiving and decoding the ROI, Machine~B performs digit segmentation using a deterministic geometry-based method. Since all target meters contain exactly five digits arranged in uniform-width drum cells, the ROI is divided into five equal vertical strips. Each strip uses a small overlap margin of approximately 2.5\% of the cell width on each side to prevent digits at cell boundaries from being clipped during the resize step.

This approach eliminates all dependency on image binarization, thresholding, or projection-based boundary detection â€” operations that are notoriously fragile under uneven illumination, glare, or partial occlusion. The only information used is the known fixed structure of the meter counter: five equal-width digit cells.

\subsection{CNN Digit Classification}
Each extracted strip is preprocessed to match the training pipeline exactly:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Resize to $20 \times 32$ pixels using bilinear interpolation (\texttt{INTER\_AREA}).
    \item Convert from BGR to RGB channel order.
    \item Cast to raw \texttt{float32} without dividing by 255 (Batch Normalization inside the CNN handles scale invariance).
\end{enumerate}

The prepared crop is then classified by a custom TensorFlow/Keras CNN trained on UFPR-AMR digit crops. The model outputs a probability distribution over 11 classes: digits 0 through 9, plus an additional \textit{N/NaN} class for unreadable or absent characters. The final reading is the concatenation of the five argmax predictions.

\subsection{Training Data and Model Setup}
The CNN was trained on 9,960 digit crops extracted from UFPR-AMR annotations. The dataset was split into 7,968 training samples and 1,992 hold-out test samples. Training was performed on raw pixel values without normalization by 255, and early stopping was used to avoid overfitting. The best model was recovered at epoch 104, while training stopped at epoch 124.

\section{Experimental Validation}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
The complete pipeline was evaluated on an independent local test set of 50 meter images. The evaluation measured both component-level performance and end-to-end reading quality.

\subsection{Component-Level Performance}
On the local set, the YOLO detector successfully localized the ROI in all 50 images, yielding a 100\% detection success rate. The average confidence score was 80.76\%, and the average IoU was 88.66\%. The CNN classifier achieved 99.00\% accuracy on the hold-out digit test set, with macro and weighted F1 scores both equal to 0.99.

Two distinct failure modes are identified:

\textbf{Hardware-level failures (Meter 1, angles $\geq 10^\circ$).}
Meter~1 failed YOLO detection at 10\textdegree\ (wrong bounding box) and produced no ROI at all at 30\textdegree\ and 45\textdegree. These failures are attributable to extreme camera tilt, not to model deficiencies. A standard axis-aligned object detector trained on upright meter images cannot be expected to reliably localize severely tilted targets. The correct mitigation is a physically stable camera mount rather than a software workaround. As a complementary safeguard, the system can emit a maintenance alert if the rotation-retry mechanism is consistently triggered, signaling potential camera drift to field operators.

\textbf{CNN recognition failures (Meters 2 and 3, high angles).}
Meter~2 produced one incorrect digit at 45\textdegree\ (\texttt{24506} instead of \texttt{22509}), and Meter~3 produced incorrect readings at 30\textdegree\ and 45\textdegree. Even at 0\textdegree, Meter~3 exhibited a digit confusion between \texttt{0} and \texttt{1} (\texttt{09738} instead of \texttt{19738}), while the reading at 10\textdegree\ was correct. These errors occur because when the camera is tilted, the YOLO-cropped ROI arrives at Machine~B in a skewed orientation. Resizing a skewed digit strip to $20 \times 32$ pixels introduces geometric distortion that causes the CNN to misclassify digits with visually similar class boundaries, such as \texttt{0} and \texttt{1}.

The three cases involving Meter~1 at angles above 0\textdegree\ represent hardware-placement failures; the remaining four partial or full errors are CNN recognition issues under tilt or low contrast. Importantly, the UFPR-AMR dataset and the Moroccan meters share the same mechanical drum-counter format, confirming that cross-distribution generalization holds under normal operating conditions.

\subsection{Error Analysis on the Local Test Set}
The gap between component-level accuracy and end-to-end reading accuracy on the local test set is mainly caused by recognition errors under challenging lighting. Strong reflections and glare on the glass surface can reduce local contrast for individual digit crops. When this occurs, the CNN may confuse visually adjacent digit classes even on correctly segmented strips. In one representative failure case from the local test set, a reading was reconstructed incorrectly due to multiple low-confidence digit predictions in a single frame affected by surface glare.

\subsection{System Limitations}
While the proposed system demonstrates strong performance on the UFPR-AMR dataset distribution, it exhibits several generalization limitations consistent with the domain shift problem in computer vision:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Novel Meter Types:} The YOLOv8 detector was trained exclusively on UFPR-AMR meters and may struggle to generalize to completely unseen meter styles, colors, or shapes.
    \item \textbf{Extreme Camera Tilt:} The rotation-retry mechanism provides a software buffer for moderate angles, but beyond approximately 20\textdegree--30\textdegree, YOLO detection degrades significantly. This is fundamentally a hardware problem: the primary mitigation is a rigid camera mount with a defined installation tolerance. A software alert triggered by persistent rotation-retry usage serves as a secondary indicator of camera drift.
    \item \textbf{Digit-Level Visual Ambiguity:} Visually similar digit classes (notably \texttt{0} vs.\ \texttt{1}) are occasionally confused under low contrast or tilt-induced distortion. This sensitivity arises from the resize step: a geometrically distorted crop loses discriminative structure that the CNN relies on.
    \item \textbf{Optical Contamination:} Dust, condensation, or blur on the protective glass can alter local contrast and cause digit-level confusion even when ROI detection is correct.
\end{itemize}
These findings highlight the trade-off between the high accuracy achieved on in-distribution data and the challenges of zero-shot generalization to unconstrained real-world environments.

\section{Conclusion and Perspectives}
This paper presented a fully automated distributed AMR system combining YOLOv8-based ROI detection with geometry-driven digit segmentation and CNN-based classification. The proposed architecture directly addresses the deployment bottleneck created by manual ROI configuration in existing edge-based workflows. By separating detection and inference across two communicating nodes, the system also offers a practical path toward scalable and modular deployment.

Experimental results show strong component-level performance, with 100\% ROI detection success on the local test set and 99.00\% digit classification accuracy. End-to-end performance reaches 72\% exact reading match on the local set, and transfer tests on Moroccan meters confirm that the UFPR-AMR training distribution generalizes well under normal operating conditions (upright or near-upright camera placement).

The primary identified failure modes are camera tilt beyond the effective range of the rotation-retry mechanism, and digit-level visual ambiguity under low contrast or geometric distortion. Both are well-understood limitations with clear mitigations. Tilt failures are a hardware-installation problem: the correct solution is a rigid camera mount and an installation quality checklist, complemented by a software alert that fires when the rotation-retry mechanism is repeatedly triggered, signaling camera drift to maintenance teams. CNN recognition errors can be addressed in future work by replacing the single-digit classifier with an end-to-end multi-output model that reads all five positions jointly from the full ROI in a single forward pass, eliminating the intermediate resize of individual strips and thereby reducing sensitivity to crop distortion. For deployments requiring support for variable digit counts or non-uniform cell spacing, a sequence modeling approach such as a CRNN with CTC loss would provide additional generalization without requiring knowledge of the digit count in advance.
